HUNDRED ISLANDS

Location

History

ACTIVITIES TO DO

Hundred Islands National Park is best explored by island hopping. Visitors can swim, snorkel, and kayak around the many islets. Popular stops include Governor's Island for its view deck and zip line, Quezon Island for its picnic areas, and Children's Island for its family-friendly shallow waters.

ROUTE AND TIPS

To get there, take a 5-6 hour bus from Manila to Alaminos City, then a tricycle to Lucap Wharf. The best time to visit is the dry season (November to May). Plan your tour early as schedules depend on the weather, and remember to bring eco-friendly essentials.

SAGADA SUMAGUING CAVE

Location

History

WHY VISIT SUMAGUING CAVE?

Sumaguing Cave is Sagada's largest and most iconic cave, famous for its incredible limestone formations. The adventure involves a challenging descent, crawling through tunnels, and wading in icy waters, making it a must for nature and adventure lovers.

ROUTE AND TIPS

Travel involves a bus from Manila to Baguio or Banaue, then a transfer to Sagada. Hire a licensed guide from the town, visit in the dry season (November-April), and wear sturdy, non-slip shoes for the wet and slippery cave interior.

VIGAN

WHAT TO DO IN VIGAN?

A visit centers on the iconic Calle Crisologo, a cobblestone street where you can ride a kalesa, explore ancestral houses, and try local food like Vigan longganisa. Don't miss the Bantay Bell Tower for views and the Plaza Salcedo dancing fountain show at night.

HISTORY OF VIGAN

Vigan is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a exceptionally well-preserved Spanish colonial town from the 16th century. Its unique architecture and cobblestone streets reflect a rich blend of Filipino, Chinese, and European cultural influences.

ROUTE AND TIPS

Vigan is an 8-10 hour bus ride from Manila. The best time to visit is the dry season (November-April). Wear comfortable shoes for walking, bring cash, and consider staying overnight to fully experience the city's historic charm by day and its lively atmosphere by night.

HISTORY OF HUNDRED ISLANDS

The islands were originally home to the Agta people, later influenced by Spanish colonization and American development. They were also used by Japanese forces during World War II before becoming the national park they are today.

123? or 124?

During high tide, only 123 islands are visible, creating the iconic seascape that the Hundred Islands National Park is known for. But when the tide recedes, a tiny additional island emerges, bringing the total count to 124.

Only 4 Developed, Hundreds Untouched

Out of the entire group, only four islands have been developed for tourism, while 30 are officially named and the rest remain in their natural, pristine condition. Together, they cover about 1,860 hectares in Alaminos, Pangasinan.

Islands 2 Million Years in the Making

Scientific studies reveal that the Hundred Islands are around 2 million years old, formed through ancient geological uplifts and marine processes. Over time, the rising seabed and the action of waves and tides sculpted the limestone formations, giving us the scattered emerald islands we see today.

HISTORY OF MT. PULAG

Mt. Pulag is a sacred mountain, revered by indigenous tribes as the resting place of ancestral souls. It was declared a national park in 1987, protecting both its rich biodiversity and deep cultural significance.

Home of the Dwarf Bamboo

The summit is uniquely covered with dwarf bamboo, a rare plant that thrives only in high-altitude environments, giving Mt. Pulag its iconic grassy peak.

One of the Coldest Place in the Philippines

Temperatures in Mt. Pulag can drop below 0°C during peak months, sometimes even forming frost on the grasslands. Mount Pulag once recorded a temperature of −2 °C on December 28, 2015, when the summit dipped below zero.

Sacred Ancestral Ground

For the Ibaloi, Ifugao, Kalanguya, and Kankanaey tribes, Mt. Pulag is believed to be the final resting place of their ancestors’ souls. Locals perform rituals and offer prayers before climbs as a sign of respect. This spiritual belief has helped protect the mountain, making it both a natural wonder and a cultural treasure.

HISTORY OF SUMAGUING CAVE

The cave was formed over millions of years by water dissolving limestone. It holds cultural significance for the local community and is considered both a natural masterpiece and an important part of the Cordillera's landscape.

Home to Thousands of Bats

Sumaguing Cave is not only a wonder for tourists but also a natural habitat for thousands of bats. Bat droppings have naturally built up over time inside Sumaguing Cave, making the steep descent at the entrance slippery—an added challenge that makes the spelunking experience even more exciting.

Formations with fun nicknames

Many of the cave’s rock formations have been given playful names by locals and guides, such as a turtle, an elephant, and even a chocolate cake, making the exploration more engaging and memorable.

Barefoot stage for safety

During the rock formation section, guides encourage visitors to go barefoot. This isn’t to make things harder—it’s because bare feet grip the rocks better than shoes, reducing the risk of slipping.

UNESCO Treasure

Vigan City, a settlement established by the Spanish in Asia during their colonial period, earned a spot on UNESCO's list in 1999 because of its architecturally distinct cityscape that fuses together influences originating from the Philippines, China, and Spain. The city was also distinguished as one of the New 7 Wonders Cities back in 2015 and is a part of the Organization of World Heritage Cities.

Pottery: Hands-On History

Vigan holds a distinguished reputation for its heritage of crafting pottery, notably the production of burnay jars, which serve as containers for both water and wine. This age-old custom, spanning across centuries, is available for observation within various workshops and offers opportunities for hands-on participation, ensuring the preservation of the craft's true essence while simultaneously supporting the endeavors of regional craftspeople.

Church Hopping

Vigan features a wealth of churches, notably the Vigan Cathedral, an impressive illustration of Baroque design. Every one of these churches has a distinct architectural style and historical background that provides insight into the area' religious heritage. Tourists can participate in masses or moments of contemplation, offering a profoundly touching experience.
